Who prescribes birth control in the United States?—

Most birth control is prescribed by people without comprehensive women's health training.

By Rebecca Rand

As A part of a well-intentioned effort to expand birth control access across the country, hormonal birth control has been adopted more and more into routine medical care. But medical organizations and researchers have sounded the alarm, repeatedly, that many primary care doctors aren't taught enough about contraceptives and women’s reproductive health in residency.

Danielle Jones, an OB-GYN and health educator, said that most providers people see don’t understand how hormonal birth control works well enough “to really be doing anything more than just basic prescribing.” This means that when patients have trouble with side effects, their providers don’t know what to do.

Today, most people on short-acting contraception get it from their primary care doctor or a nurse practitioner.

As of 2019, there were more than twice as many Primary Care providers prescribing the Pill, Patch, or Ring compared to women's health doctors (i.e. OB-GYNs).
